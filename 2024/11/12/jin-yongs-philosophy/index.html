Jin Yong’s philosophy

Created2024-11-12|Updated2024-11-16|个人见解
|Post Views:

Jin Yong’s works often feature characters who resist oppressive regimes, challenge corruption, and stand against unjust authority figures. This recurring theme reflects both the social and political climate of Hong Kong during his writing period, as well as Jin Yong’s personal values and philosophies.

1. The Political Climate of Hong Kong

Jin Yong wrote his novels between the 1950s and 1970s, when Hong Kong was a British colony. The Chinese population in Hong Kong lived under British rule, with limited political rights and a notable wealth gap. While the colonial government offered relative stability, Hong Kong residents had no significant say in political matters. Jin Yong was acutely aware of these realities and focused on themes of justice and resistance, likely reflecting a desire for fairness and freedom within Hong Kong society.

Furthermore, this period also coincided with significant political upheaval in mainland China, including the Cultural Revolution. Many people who were discontented with the situation in China fled to Hong Kong. Against this backdrop, a spirit of resistance and a longing for justice resonated strongly among intellectuals in Hong Kong, including Jin Yong. His works echo these sentiments, highlighting characters who embody the courage to challenge oppression.

2. Jin Yong’s Personal Ideals and Values

Jin Yong’s philosophy was deeply influenced by traditional Chinese values, particularly Confucian, Daoist, and Buddhist teachings. He valued the concept of “xia” (侠), or chivalry, which emphasizes social responsibility, justice, and selflessness. His characters often risk their lives to defend the vulnerable and stand against injustice, embodying this “chivalric spirit.” Characters like Guo Jing and Xiao Feng, despite being independent from any governing authority, dedicate themselves to the well-being of the people, reflecting a sense of duty toward society rather than personal gain.

The Contrast Between Chivalry and Authority In Jin Yong’s works, the chivalric ideal frequently conflicts with corrupt or inept authorities. This contrast underscores his cautious view of power. Jin Yong believed that power easily leads to corruption, so he favored heroes who operate outside formal structures, wielding justice without relying on authority. Through these characters, he conveyed his ideals of “benevolence and righteousness” while maintaining a critical perspective on political power.

In Summary

Jin Yong’s portrayal of rebels and resistance to tyranny reflects Hong Kong’s own struggles for justice and freedom during his time. His works express his ideals of social justice and his distrust of authority through complex heroes who resist oppression. Ultimately, Jin Yong’s novels go beyond the martial arts genre—they serve as a critique of human nature, a commentary on power, and an exploration of ideals for a just society.
